Build breakdown

The geometry that mattered

The investigation centered on understanding feedpoint position, stub length, capacitive gap, and spacing as a system rather than as isolated dimensions.

Parallel wire spacing

0.5 inch spacing

The parallel section acts like a two-wire transmission line. In this build, about 0.5 inch spacing with 14 AWG wire gave a usable impedance transformation profile and let the feedpoint pass through the 50Ω region during tuning.

Capacitive gap

Fine reactance control

The bottom gap was reduced from 1.0 inch to 0.5 inch, increasing capacitance and helping cancel residual inductive reactance after the larger tuning moves were made.

Feedpoint position

Primary resistance control

Raising the feedpoint from 1.5 inches to 3 inches moved resistance toward the desired 50Ω region. This was the strongest and most immediate impedance-matching adjustment.

Air choke

Common-mode suppression

A 4 inch diameter air-wound choke with 0.25 inch turn spacing was added to suppress common-mode current, reduce feedline radiation, and stabilize measurements.

Measurement results

Tuning, then validating in deployment

The antenna was measured directly at the feedpoint during tuning and then again through a 15 meter coax run at the transmitter to compare true antenna behavior against system-level behavior.

Initial build
33.28 + j28.6 Ω
SWR 2.6

Low resistance and positive reactance indicated a feedpoint that was too low and an antenna that was still electrically short.

Tuned configuration
56.31 − j8.02 Ω
SWR 1.21

After moving the feedpoint, extending the stub, and reducing the gap, the system landed near the target with only slight capacitive reactance.

Deployed at transmitter
49.15 + j4.42 Ω
SWR 1.095

Measured through 15 meters of coax, the system appears closer to ideal — but that improvement is partly the result of impedance rotation and loss masking in the feedline.

Key takeaways

What the build actually taught

The build wasn’t just about getting a low SWR. It was about understanding how geometry, measurement location, and transmission-line effects change what the antenna seems to be doing.

Feedpoint dominates resistance

The fastest way to move toward a 50Ω match was raising the feedpoint.

Stub length controls resonance

Lengthening the stub lowered resonance and reduced inductive reactance.

Gap fine-tunes reactance

The capacitive gap acted as a small correction after the main tuning moves.

Coax can mislead

Long coax can rotate impedance and hide mismatch, so direct feedpoint measurement still matters.
