Engineering foundations

Principles that shape how systems are designed, tuned, and debugged.

The tools change from project to project, but the underlying problems are usually recognizable. A hydraulic axis, a robot cell, and a distributed software service can all fail for different reasons while still reflecting the same deeper issues in control, timing, visibility, and stability.

Control systems and dynamics

Practical application of control theory across hydraulic and electromechanical systems, including response tuning, feed-forward behavior, and the tradeoffs between speed, stability, and robustness.

System identification

Working from measured behavior instead of ideal assumptions. Familiar with time constants, delays, coupling, and nonlinear effects that influence how real machines respond under load.

Distributed and real-time systems

Designing communication between services, PLCs, robots, and vision systems where sequencing, message integrity, and failure recovery are critical to overall system behavior.

Signal, measurement, and filtering

Experience working with noisy signals, real sensors, and imperfect measurements across industrial instrumentation and RF systems, with attention to filtering, observability, and interpretation of data.

Hydraulic and physical systems

Understanding how force, pressure, flow, friction, inertia, and compliance affect machine behavior, and how those physical constraints influence control strategy and expected performance.

Field debugging

Systems rarely fail in clean ways. Much of the real work is tracing interactions between software, controls, electrical hardware, mechanics, and process conditions until the actual source of behavior is exposed.

Approach

How the work is typically approached.

The goal is usually not just to make a feature work once. It is to make a system understandable, supportable, and resilient enough to survive deployment, handoff, and production reality.

System-first thinking

Looking beyond individual components to understand how behavior emerges from the whole system.

Production-minded design

Building with deployment, troubleshooting, maintainability, and operator visibility in mind.

Cross-functional integration

Working across software, controls, electrical systems, technicians, and field teams to close gaps between disciplines.

Measured troubleshooting

Using instrumentation, logs, tests, and observed behavior to narrow problems instead of guessing from symptoms alone.
